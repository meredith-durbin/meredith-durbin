Here we see the whole sky in six different parts of the electromagnetic spectrum, from gamma rays to radio, each of which tells us something different about the cosmos. Gamma and x-rays tell us about high-energy phenomena such as supernovae, hot gas, and black holes; visible light shows us most main-sequence stars; the infrared reveals star-forming regions, dust, and high-redshift objects; microwaves map out the background radiation from the Big Bang (as you'll see further down), and radio (specifically a wavelength of 21 cm) tells us about the distribution of neutral hydrogen in the universe. The approximate wavelengths of each type of radiation are on the top right.

the universe today

quasars

Quasars are some of the most luminous objects ever observed. They are thought to be supermassive black holes at the cores of young galaxies, producing up to trillions of times the energy of the sun as matter accretes around them and heats up as it falls inward. The discovery of large numbers of quasars in the early universe was one of the first strong pieces of evidence in favor of the Big Bang theory rather than steady state cosmology, as it proved that characteristics of the universe changed over time.

the first galaxies

This is the deepest image of space ever taken, assembled from 10 years' worth of Hubble observations with a total exposure time of over 22 days, and showing galaxies as far back as 600 million years after the Big Bang. The smaller and redder a galaxy is, the older it is.
One of the missions of the James Webb Space Telescope, due to launch in 2018, will be to study the earliest galaxies. JWST will be able to expose an image comparable to the Hubble deep field in a matter of hours.

the first stars

The first stars were nothing like the stars we see today—they were up to several hundred times the mass of the sun, and lived less than a few million years (compare that to our sun's current age of about 5 billion years). They were the first objects to fuse significant amounts of elements heavier than helium, and dispersed these elements throughout the universe when their lives ended in supernovae.
The cosmic microwave background is a precise marker of the age at which the universe became cool enough for electrons and nuclei to combine into atoms, leaving radiation free to propagate through the universe rather than scatter off free electrons. We see the CMB today at a temperature of 2.73 K and wavelength of 1.063 nm, redshifted by a factor of 1100 from when it originated. These three all-sky maps are what happens when you subtract out the Milky Way from a microwave map of the sky. The variations in color are miniscule (~10-5 K) deviations from the average temperature; as technology has progressed over the past twenty years, we can resolve more and more detail in the CMB.

nucleosynthesis

Within the first few minutes of the universe's birth, atomic nuclei began to form. Protons and neutrons combined into deuterium, which then fused into tritium and helium, eventually finishing when the universe was 25% helium by mass with traces of other elements and the rest remaining hydrogen. The amount of helium formed places tight constraints on the temperature and density of the universe at this time—at the start of nucleosynthesis, the temperature of the entire universe was comparable to the center of the sun.

inflation

Just a fraction of a second after the universe was born, it underwent a dramatic exponential expansion, referred to as the inflationary epoch. Inflation explains how the universe today is homogeneous on the largest scales: if the universe were extremely small just before inflation, everything inside it had time to come into thermal equilibrium before expansion, explaining how the CMB temperature is smooth to one part in 10-5. It also explains how the universe appears spatially flat today: even if space were curved at the beginning, inflation would quickly smooth it out, similar to blowing up a balloon.
As string theorist Brian Greene quipped in The Fabric of the Cosmos, the Big Bang theory leaves out the bang. The actual instant of the Big Bang, the singularity of infinite temperature and density, is fundamentally unknowable, because it predates spacetime and the laws of physics themselves, and understanding the era immediately after requires a formulation of quantum gravity that current physics has yet to arrive at. There are many alternative theories that do away with the need for a singularity, but unfortunately none are testable. The nature of the Big Bang may be the closest thing there is to a fundamental limit of human knowledge, but that's certainly never stopped scientists from trying.
