our place in the galaxy

Just as the Earth does not occupy a privileged position in the solar system, neither does the solar system occupy a privileged position in the Milky Way. We live about two-thirds of the way out from the galactic center (that's about 27,000 light years) in the inner edge of the Orion Armâ€”or, as Douglas Adams puts it in The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy, "the uncharted backwaters of the unfashionable end of the western spiral arm of the Galaxy".

the galactic center

At the center of our galaxy, and many others, there lurks a cosmic beast: a black hole, an object so gravitationally powerful that even light cannot escape it. Based on the orbits of the surrounding stars, this black hole, dubbed Sagittarius A*, is estimated to have a mass of at least 4 million times that of the sun. The Milky Way is not only made of stars; there's a good deal of stuff hanging out in between and around the stars too. (One atom per cubic centimeter on average is a good deal when it fills an entire galaxy.) This image is from the GLIMPSE survey in the infrared, The dark regions are molecular dust that absorbs light, the pink bubbles are hotter ionized gas that has been blown off by young stars, and the pink and yellow point sources are stars.

star-forming regions

When dense clouds of the interstellar medium reach the right conditions, the cloud will begin to collapse into a star. Just such a process is pictured here; a star is being born in the center of the red dust cloud. The Milky Way is still undergoing active star formation, mainly in its spiral arms. This is why the spiral arms are bluer on average than the central bar and bulge; the arms are full of young, hot stars.
In 2010, researchers using the Fermi gamma-ray telescope discovered what appeared to be two large "bubbles" extending about 25,000 light years above and below the plane of the Milky Way. There are several theories as to what caused these: they may be relics of an ancient jet from the central black hole; they may be left over from gas outflow during a previous burst of star formation; or, most mysteriously, they may be the result of collisions between dark matter particles that lead to the production of extremely high-energy particles of visible matter.

satellite galaxies

Our galaxy has many companions surrounding it: small bundles of anywhere from several billion to only a thousand stars. (The Milky Way itself contains several hundred billion stars.) Several of these dwarf galaxies are caught in orbit around the Milky Way. Pictured here are the only two companion galaxies visible to the naked eye, the Large and Small Magellanic Clouds.
Most of the mass in the universe, the Milky Way included, is invisible to usâ€”this is the mysterious dark matter that may be responsible for the Fermi bubbles we saw previously. The amount of dark matter in a galaxy can be calculated by measuring the orbital velocities of stars at different radii from the center, resulting in what is known as a rotation curve (plotted above), and then comparing the real data with the predicted values from the distribution of visible matter alone. The Milky Way may be up to 90% dark matter by some estimates.
